Do I Owe Taxes If I Made Less Than $600 on a Gig?

Short answer: yes. The $600 rule is about 1099 forms — not taxes. Here's exactly what you owe, when you owe it, and what happens if you don't report it.

Yes — You Still Owe Taxes

The $600 threshold is for 1099 forms only. The IRS has a completely different — and lower — threshold for when you must pay tax.

This is one of the most common and costly misconceptions in gig work. Millions of people believe that if a platform doesn't send them a 1099 form, they don't owe taxes. That is incorrect. The 1099 form is just a paperwork requirement — the tax obligation exists regardless of whether you receive one.

This is just a paperwork threshold. It determines when DoorDash, Uber, or your client must mail you a form. It has nothing to do with your tax obligation.

$400
This is the actual tax filing threshold. Net self-employment income over $400 means you must file Schedule SE and pay self-employment tax — no matter what.
⚠️ The $400 IRS threshold applies to net income — that's after your business expenses. If you earned $800 delivering for DoorDash but spent $450 on gas and supplies, your net is $350 — below the threshold. But if you earned $500 with minimal expenses, you likely owe tax.
The IRS Rule

The $600 vs. $400 Rule — Explained Simply

There are two completely separate rules that gig workers confuse constantly. Here's how they actually work:

$600
When a platform must send you a 1099-NEC form
$400
When the IRS requires you to file and pay SE tax
$0
Minimum income you should report (technically all of it)

Rule #1 — The 1099-NEC Form Threshold ($600)

When you earn $600 or more from a single client or platform in a calendar year, that payer is required by law to send you (and the IRS) a Form 1099-NEC by January 31 of the following year. This is purely an information-reporting requirement placed on the payer. It's there so the IRS can track payments.

If you earn $599 from a client, they are not required to send you a 1099. But that doesn't mean you don't owe taxes on that $599.

Rule #2 — The Self-Employment Tax Filing Threshold ($400)

Separately and independently, the IRS requires you to report and pay self-employment tax if your net self-employment income is $400 or more for the year. "Net" means after you subtract your business expenses from your gross income.

This rule applies:

  • Whether or not you received a 1099 form
  • Whether you earned the money from one source or twenty
  • Whether you got paid in cash, Venmo, PayPal, or bank transfer
  • Whether it was a one-time gig or ongoing work
  • Even if it was a side hustle alongside a W-2 job
💡 The rules work independently. A platform may not send you a 1099 (because you earned under $600 from them) while you still owe taxes on that income. The IRS expects you to know this and comply — ignorance of the rule is not a defense in an audit.

Tax Filing

What Forms Do You File If You Made Under $600?

If you earned over $400 net but never received a 1099, you still file the same forms as someone who did receive one. The difference is you enter the numbers yourself rather than copying from a form. Here's what you need:

1

Form 1040 — Your Main Tax Return

Everyone files this. Your self-employment income (with or without a 1099) flows onto Schedule 1 of Form 1040. You report your gross income and expenses here.

2

Schedule C — Profit or Loss from Business

You list your gross gig income on Schedule C, then subtract your business expenses (mileage, phone, supplies, etc.) to arrive at net profit. This number flows to your 1040. Even with no 1099, you self-report your income here.

3

Schedule SE — Self-Employment Tax

If your Schedule C net profit is $400+, you fill out Schedule SE. This calculates your 15.3% self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare). You pay this on top of regular income tax.

✅ Good news: Most tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxSlayer) will walk you through this automatically. Just enter your income when prompted, even without a 1099. The software fills out Schedule C and Schedule SE for you.

How to Document Income With No 1099

Without a 1099, you're self-reporting. The IRS accepts this — they just expect you to keep records in case of an audit. Keep:

  • Bank statements showing deposits from gig platforms or clients
  • Payment receipts or earnings statements from your app (Uber, DoorDash, etc. all let you download these)
  • PayPal, Venmo, or Cash App transaction history if paid that way
  • Invoices you sent to clients
  • A simple spreadsheet log of income by date and source

You don't submit these with your return — just keep them for 3 years in case the IRS asks.

Important Warning

What Happens If You Don't Report Under-$600 Income?

Many people assume that because they received no 1099, the IRS won't know about the income. That assumption is risky for several reasons:

The IRS Gets More Information Than You Think

Even when platforms don't send you a 1099-NEC, they often still report aggregate payment data to the IRS through other channels. Bank records, payment processors, and platform data can all surface in an audit. The IRS has significantly increased its matching technology in recent years.

Audits Can Look at Your Entire Return

If the IRS audits your return for any reason (an error elsewhere, a random selection, a mismatch with a W-2), they look at everything — including unreported cash income. Small amounts can trigger penalties and back taxes with interest.

The Penalty Structure

  • Failure to file penalty: 5% of unpaid taxes per month, up to 25%
  • Failure to pay penalty: 0.5% per month of unpaid taxes
  • Interest: Currently ~8% per year on unpaid amounts
  • Accuracy-related penalty: 20% of the underpayment for negligence or disregard of rules
🚨 Tax evasion is a federal crime — even on small amounts. The IRS generally distinguishes between honest mistakes (which result in back taxes + interest) and intentional concealment (which can result in criminal charges). Always report all income and let your deductions do the work of reducing your bill legally.

The Right Approach: Report Everything, Deduct What You Can

The IRS wants you to pay tax on your net profit — not gross income. That means you subtract every legitimate business expense before your tax is calculated. If you earned $500 driving for Uber but spent $200 on gas and maintenance, you only owe tax on $300 net — which is under the $400 threshold anyway. Smart deduction tracking is legal, ethical, and dramatically reduces your bill.
