Understanding Tax Brackets

How Federal Tax Brackets Actually Work

The biggest tax myth in America: "If I earn more, I'll pay a higher rate on ALL my income." This is completely false. The US uses a progressive tax system — you only pay the higher rate on income above each threshold.

⚡ Marginal Tax Rate

The rate that applies to your last dollar of income. This is the bracket you "fall into." If you're in the 22% bracket, your marginal rate is 22% — but only on income above $48,475. Everything below that threshold is taxed at lower rates.

📊 Effective Tax Rate

The average rate you actually pay across all your income. Always lower than your marginal rate. A person in the 22% bracket typically has an effective rate of 12–15%. This is the number that truly reflects your tax burden.

💡 Example: A single filer with $65,000 in taxable income is in the 22% bracket — but they pay 10% on the first $11,925, 12% on income from $11,926–$48,475, and only 22% on the remaining $16,525 above $48,475. Their total federal tax is approximately $9,200, giving an effective rate of about 14.1% — not 22%.

1099 Workers Specifically

Tax Brackets for Gig Workers — What's Different

As a 1099 worker, you pay the same federal income tax brackets as everyone else — but you also owe self-employment tax on top. Here's the complete picture:

  • SE Tax (15.3%): Calculated on 92.35% of your net profit, paid before income tax is considered. You deduct half of SE tax from gross income before calculating income tax.
  • Standard deduction ($15,000 single, 2026): Reduces your taxable income further.
  • Business deductions: Mileage, home office, equipment — all reduce net profit before both SE tax and income tax.
  • QBI deduction: Many gig workers can deduct up to 20% of qualified business income, potentially dropping them a full bracket.
✅ Strategy tip: If your taxable income is just above a bracket threshold (e.g., $50,000 when the 22% bracket starts at $48,476), maxing your SEP-IRA or deducting additional business expenses could push you back into the 12% bracket — saving you 10 cents on every dollar above the threshold.

Common Questions

Tax Bracket FAQ

No — this is the most common tax myth in America. The US uses a marginal tax system. Only the income above each threshold is taxed at the higher rate. If you're a single filer and earn $50,000 taxable income, you pay 10% on the first $11,925, 12% on the next $36,550, and only 22% on the last $1,525 above $48,475. Earning more never makes your total tax bill go down — but it also doesn't mean suddenly paying 22% on everything.
Gross income is everything you earn. Taxable income is what the IRS actually taxes you on — after deductions. For 1099 workers: start with gross income, subtract business expenses (mileage, home office, equipment), subtract half of SE tax, subtract the standard deduction ($15,000 for single filers in 2026). The result is taxable income. A freelancer earning $80,000 gross with $20,000 in deductions has only $60,000 in taxable income — putting them in a much lower bracket than their gross suggests.
The standard deduction reduces your taxable income before applying brackets. For 2026, it's $15,000 for single filers, $30,000 for married filing jointly, and $22,500 for head of household. This is in addition to business deductions — you subtract business expenses first to get net profit, then subtract the standard deduction from net profit to get taxable income. Both apply.
The brackets are adjusted annually for inflation. The 2026 brackets shown here reflect IRS inflation adjustments — each threshold is slightly higher than 2025, meaning you can earn more before crossing into a higher bracket. The rates themselves (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) remain the same as established by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act. These rates are scheduled to remain through 2025 and discussions around TCJA extension may affect 2026+ rates — check IRS.gov for the most current official guidance.
Several strategies can reduce your taxable income and potentially drop you into a lower bracket: (1) Maximize business deductions — mileage, home office, equipment, software; (2) Contribute to a SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) — contributions reduce net profit dollar-for-dollar; (3) Claim the QBI deduction (up to 20% of net business income); (4) Deduct health insurance premiums in full if self-employed; (5) Time large income or deductions strategically across years.
