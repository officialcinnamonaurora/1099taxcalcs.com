Reduce Your Tax Bill

Top Tax Deductions for Freelancers

Unlike gig workers, freelancers have more varied and often larger deductions. Every dollar of deductions reduces both your SE tax and income tax.

🏠
Home Office
Up to $1,500/year (simplified)
Dedicated workspace = $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft. Or use actual expenses method for larger deduction. Must be used exclusively for work.
💻
Equipment & Hardware
100% deductible (Section 179)
Laptop, monitors, keyboard, webcam, microphone, external drives — any hardware used for your freelance work. Deduct the full cost in year of purchase.
🔧
Software & Subscriptions
100% deductible
Adobe CC, Figma, GitHub, Notion, Slack, Zoom, project management tools — any software you use for client work. Monthly or annual fees all deductible.
🌐
Internet & Phone
Business % deductible
If you work from home, deduct 50–80% of your internet bill. Phone bill business percentage is also deductible. Add up to $1,500–$2,000/year in deductions.
💊
Health Insurance
100% of premiums
If freelancing is your primary income and you buy your own insurance, deduct 100% of premiums for yourself, spouse, and dependents. Often $5,000–$15,000/year.
📚
Education & Courses
100% if work-related
Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, books, conferences — any education that improves skills in your current field is deductible. Not for switching careers.
💰
SEP-IRA Contributions
Up to $70,000/year in 2026
Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income to a SEP-IRA. Fully deductible and reduces both income tax and SE tax base. Biggest tax lever available.
🌍
Platform Fees
100% deductible
Upwork, Fiverr, and other platform fees are deductible business expenses. Deduct them on Schedule C — they reduce your net profit and therefore your SE tax.

Even without a form, you are legally required to report all freelance income over $400 net. Upwork does not withhold taxes — you pay them yourself quarterly.

In that case, you need to separately deduct the fee on your tax return. Keep records of all fees paid. TurboTax Self-Employed handles this automatically if you input it correctly.
As a Fiverr seller, you owe SE tax (15.3%) and income tax on all your Fiverr earnings minus Fiverr's 20% fee and any other business expenses. Fiverr will send you a 1099-K if you earned $5,000+ (or $600+ starting 2026). Make quarterly estimated payments to the IRS at IRS.gov/payments to avoid penalties. Use our calculator above — select "Fiverr — 20%" as your platform fee.
Yes — if you have a dedicated workspace used exclusively and regularly for work. The simplified method: $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft = maximum $1,500/year deduction. The actual expense method can yield larger deductions if you rent or have a large home — deduct the percentage of your home used for work × actual home expenses (rent, utilities, insurance). Most freelancers use the simplified method for ease.
A SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) are the two best options. SEP-IRA: contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, maximum $70,000 for 2026. Easy to set up at Fidelity or Vanguard in 20 minutes. Solo 401(k): higher contribution limits if you want to max out — up to $70,000 combined employee + employer contributions. Both reduce your taxable income dollar for dollar. A $20,000 SEP-IRA contribution saves roughly $5,000–$7,000 in taxes depending on your bracket.
For most freelancers earning under $80,000/year: an LLC adds legal protection but doesn't save taxes — you still pay SE tax on all profits. Above $80,000–$100,000: an S-Corp election can save significant SE tax by splitting income into salary + distributions. The salary portion pays SE tax; distributions do not. The savings can be $5,000–$15,000/year but comes with added complexity (payroll, separate tax return). Consult a CPA before making this decision.
