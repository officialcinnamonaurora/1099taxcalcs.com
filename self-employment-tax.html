What Is Self-Employment Tax? 15.3% SE Tax Explained

Self-employment tax is the Social Security and Medicare tax that every 1099 worker, freelancer, and gig worker pays. Here's exactly what it is, who pays it, how to calculate it, and how to legally reduce it — explained in plain English.

Section 1

What Is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax (commonly called SE tax) is the combined Social Security and Medicare tax that self-employed people pay. When you work a traditional W-2 job, these taxes are split equally between you and your employer — you each pay 7.65%. When you're self-employed, there is no employer to cover the other half, so you pay the entire 15.3% yourself.

This is often the biggest financial surprise for new gig workers and freelancers. They know about income tax. They don't always know about SE tax sitting on top of it. A freelancer in the 22% income tax bracket who earns $60,000 net profit owes roughly $8,480 in SE tax before a single dollar of income tax is calculated.

Section 2

Who Has to Pay Self-Employment Tax?

You owe SE tax if your net self-employment earnings are $400 or more during a tax year. This applies regardless of age, whether you have a regular job, or whether you received a 1099 form. The IRS looks at net profit — your earnings after business expenses — not your gross income.

Who must pay SE tax:

  • Gig workers: Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, Amazon Flex, Grubhub drivers
  • Freelancers: Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal, direct client work
  • Independent contractors of any kind
  • Sole proprietors and single-member LLC owners
  • People with side hustle income alongside a W-2 job
  • Anyone receiving a 1099-NEC or 1099-K
  • Cash-based self-employed workers (even without a 1099)
⚠️ Side hustle with a W-2 job If you earn $500 driving for DoorDash on weekends while also working a full-time job, you owe SE tax on the DoorDash net profit. Your W-2 withholding does NOT cover your gig income. You'll likely owe extra at tax time — or need to make quarterly estimated payments.

Who does NOT pay SE tax:

  • W-2 employees (your employer handles FICA withholding)
  • People earning less than $400 in net self-employment income
  • S-Corp shareholders on distribution income (only salary portion pays SE tax)
  • Passive investors receiving rental income (with some exceptions)
  • People receiving Social Security or pension income only
Section 3

The 15.3% Rate — What It Covers

The 15.3% SE tax rate is not a single tax — it's two separate programs combined:

📊 SE Tax Breakdown — 15.3% Total

Social Security: 12.4%
Medicare: 2.9%
Social Security (12.4%) — capped at $176,100 wages in 2026
Medicare (2.9%) — no earnings cap

Social Security portion: 12.4%

The 12.4% Social Security tax applies to your net self-employment earnings up to the Social Security wage base, which is $176,100 for 2026. If you earn more than this, you stop paying the 12.4% portion on income above that threshold (though Medicare continues on all earnings).

For most gig workers and freelancers earning under $176,100, the full 12.4% applies to every dollar of net profit.

Medicare portion: 2.9%

The 2.9% Medicare tax applies to all your net self-employment earnings with no cap. High earners (above $200,000 for single filers) also pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax, bringing their Medicare rate to 3.8% on the amount above the threshold.

✅ The 92.35% rule The IRS doesn't apply SE tax to 100% of your net profit. Instead, they apply it to 92.35% (0.9235) of net profit. This is a built-in adjustment that partially compensates for the fact that employees only pay SE tax on their gross wages, while the employer's matching contribution comes out of business operating costs. The result: you pay slightly less SE tax than if the full 100% were used.
Section 4

How to Calculate SE Tax Step by Step

The IRS formula for SE tax is straightforward. Here it is broken into clear steps:

// STEP 1: Calculate net profit
Net Profit = Gross 1099 Income − Business Expenses

// STEP 2: Apply 92.35% adjustment
SE Base = Net Profit × 0.9235

// STEP 3: Apply 15.3% SE tax rate
SE Tax = SE Base × 0.153

// STEP 4: Deduct half of SE tax from income (reduces income tax)
SE Tax Deduction = SE Tax × 0.5

// Total SE Tax = Step 3 result — reported on Schedule SE

SE Tax vs Income Tax — The Key Difference

Many people confuse SE tax with income tax, or assume they're the same thing. They are completely separate taxes calculated on different bases. You owe both.

Section 7

The Half SE Tax Deduction — How It Works

Here's one of the few breaks the IRS gives self-employed workers: you can deduct 50% of your SE tax from your gross income before calculating income tax. This is an "above-the-line" deduction — meaning it reduces your income regardless of whether you itemize or take the standard deduction.

// After calculating SE tax:
SE Tax Deduction = SE Tax × 0.5

// This reduces your income BEFORE the standard deduction:
Adjusted Gross Income = Net Profit − SE Tax Deduction
Taxable Income = AGI − Standard Deduction

// Result: Your income tax bill is lower because of SE tax you paid

On $10,000 in SE tax, you deduct $5,000 from your income. If you're in the 22% bracket, that saves you $1,100 in income tax. It doesn't eliminate the SE tax burden — but it partially compensates for it.

✅ This happens automatically Tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block, TaxSlayer) and Schedule SE both handle this deduction automatically. You don't need to calculate or enter it separately — just ensure your Schedule C and Schedule SE are complete.
Section 8

How to Reduce Your SE Tax Legally

SE tax is calculated on your net profit — not your gross income. Every legitimate business deduction reduces your net profit, which directly reduces your SE tax bill. Here are the most powerful strategies:

🚗

Track Every Business Mile

Saves $1,530 per 10,000 miles

Mileage at 72.5¢/mile reduces net profit. 10,000 miles = $7,250 deduction × 15.3% SE rate = $1,109 saved in SE tax alone.

🏠

Home Office Deduction

Saves up to $230 in SE tax

Up to $1,500 deduction (simplified method). Reduces both SE tax and income tax. Easy to claim — just a dedicated workspace.

💊

Deduct Health Insurance

Saves $765–$2,295 in SE tax

100% of health insurance premiums reduce net profit if self-employed. On $5,000 premiums — saves $765 in SE tax.

🏦

SEP-IRA Contribution

Up to $10,710 in SE tax savings

Contribute up to 25% of net profit (max $70,000). Every $10,000 contributed saves $1,530 in SE tax. Open in 20 min at Fidelity.

💻

Deduct Equipment & Software

Saves 15.3% of each deduction

Laptop, camera, software subscriptions — all reduce net profit. A $2,000 laptop saves $306 in SE tax through Section 179.

🏢

S-Corp Election (high earners)

Can save $5,000–$15,000+/year

Above ~$80,000 net profit, electing S-Corp status splits income into salary + distributions. Only salary portion pays SE tax. Consult a CPA first.

⚠️ The S-Corp strategy has real costs: You need to run payroll, file a separate corporate return (Form 1120-S), and pay yourself a "reasonable salary." The added complexity costs $1,000–$3,000/year in accounting fees. It only makes financial sense above roughly $80,000 in net profit.
Section 9

How and When to Pay SE Tax

SE tax is not paid separately from income tax. You pay both together through the same quarterly estimated payment process and on your annual return. Here's how it works:

Quarterly estimated payments (if you expect to owe $1,000+)

Pay at IRS.gov/payments using IRS Direct Pay — free, no account needed. Select "Estimated Tax" and tax year 2026. Pay the combined SE tax + income tax estimate divided by 4.

QuarterIncome PeriodDue Date
Q1Jan–MarApril 15, 2026
Q2Apr–MayJune 16, 2026
Q3Jun–AugSeptember 15, 2026 ← Next deadline
Q4Sep–DecJanuary 15, 2027

Annual filing (Schedule SE)

At tax time (April 15), you file Schedule SE attached to your Form 1040. This form calculates the exact SE tax amount based on your Schedule C net profit. Tax software generates it automatically. Any remaining balance after quarterly payments is due April 15; overpayments become refunds.

✅ Pro tip: If you haven't set aside money for taxes yet, don't panic. Calculate your total estimate using our SE tax calculator, then make a large Q3 payment by September 15 to get as close to the safe harbor as possible. The underpayment penalty is only ~8% annually — not the end of the world, but avoidable.
