Schedule C Explained — Line by Line

Section 1

What Is Schedule C?

Schedule C, officially titled "Profit or Loss from Business," is the IRS form where sole proprietors — including gig workers, freelancers, and independent contractors — report their business income and expenses. It calculates your net profit or loss, which is the foundation for everything else in your tax return.

Think of Schedule C as your business's mini income statement. Part I totals your income. Part II totals your expenses. The difference is your net profit — the number that determines your self-employment tax (via Schedule SE) and contributes to your overall taxable income (via Form 1040).

💡 Schedule C is not optional paperwork If you're self-employed with any gig income, Schedule C isn't an extra form you can skip — it's the required method for reporting that income. Most tax software (TurboTax, H&R Block) builds Schedule C automatically as you answer questions about your gig work.
Section 2

Who Needs to File Schedule C?

You need to file Schedule C if you operated a business as a sole proprietor or single-member LLC during the tax year and had income or expenses to report. This includes:

  • Gig drivers: Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, Amazon Flex
  • Freelancers: Upwork, Fiverr, direct clients
  • Independent consultants of any kind
  • Online sellers (Etsy, eBay) operating as a business
  • Side hustlers with any self-employment income over $400 net

You file one Schedule C per business. If you drive for multiple delivery and rideshare apps, that's typically one business (transportation services) and one Schedule C. If you also freelance as a graphic designer on the side, that's a genuinely separate business and needs its own Schedule C.

Section 4

Part II — Expenses (Lines 8–28)

This is where you list every business deduction. Each line is a specific expense category. Here are the lines most relevant to gig workers and freelancers:

Line 9
Car & truck expenses

Mileage deduction or actual vehicle expenses. Your single biggest deduction as a driver.

Line 11
Contract labor

If you paid someone else to help with your business (rare for most gig workers).

Line 13
Depreciation/Section 179

Equipment purchases like laptops, cameras — full cost deducted in year of purchase.

Line 15
Insurance (business)

Business liability insurance, NOT health insurance (that goes on Schedule 1 instead).

Line 17
Legal & professional fees

Tax software cost, CPA fees, legal consultation fees related to your business.

Line 18
Office expense

Supplies, small equipment, printer paper, pens — general office consumables.

Line 20b
Rent — other business property

Equipment rental, storage unit for business inventory or supplies.

Line 22
Supplies

Delivery bags, packaging materials, work-specific supplies and consumables.

Line 23
Taxes & licenses

Business license fees, platform registration fees, local business taxes.

Line 24a
Travel

Business travel away from your tax home (not regular daily driving — that's line 9).

Line 25
Utilities

Business-percentage of phone bill, internet, if not claimed on home office worksheet.

Line 27a
Other expenses

Software subscriptions, platform fees (Upwork, Fiverr), bank fees, education courses — itemized on Part V.

Line 28 — Total Expenses

This is the sum of all expense lines above. This number gets subtracted from your gross income (Line 7) to calculate your tentative profit.

Line 30 — Home Office Deduction

Calculated separately on Form 8829 (or the simplified method worksheet) and entered here. The simplified method is $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft, for a maximum $1,500 deduction.

Line 31 — Net Profit or Loss

This is the most important number on the entire form: Line 7 (gross income) − Line 28 (total expenses) − Line 30 (home office) = Line 31 (net profit). This flows to Schedule SE for self-employment tax and to Form 1040 for income tax.

Section 5

Part III, IV, V — Cost of Goods, Vehicle Info, Other Expenses

Part III — Cost of Goods Sold

Only relevant if you sell physical products (Etsy shop, reselling business). Most service-based gig workers and freelancers leave this blank or enter $0 on Line 4. If you do sell goods, this section calculates your inventory costs.

Part IV — Information on Your Vehicle

If you claimed car/truck expenses on Line 9, Part IV asks detailed questions: when you placed the vehicle in service, total miles driven, business miles, commuting miles, and whether you have written evidence (a mileage log) to support your deduction. This section is exactly why mileage tracking apps like Stride or MileIQ matter — you need defensible records here.

Part V — Other Expenses

This is where you itemize anything that doesn't fit a specific line in Part II. List each expense type and amount, then total it and carry the total to Line 27a. Common Part V entries for gig workers:

  • Mileage tracking app subscription (Stride Premium, MileIQ, Everlance)
  • Platform fees (Upwork service fee, Fiverr commission)
  • Bank fees on business account
  • Continuing education or skill-building courses
  • Business-related subscriptions (Canva, Adobe, project management tools)

Section 7

How Schedule C Connects to Schedule SE and Form 1040

Schedule C doesn't exist in isolation — it's the starting point for several other parts of your tax return:

FormWhat It DoesConnection to Schedule C
Schedule SECalculates 15.3% self-employment taxUses Schedule C Line 31 (net profit) as its starting point
Schedule 1Reports additional income and adjustmentsSchedule C net profit flows here, plus the ½ SE tax deduction
Form 1040Your main tax returnSchedule 1 total flows into your overall taxable income
Form 8995 (QBI)Qualified Business Income deductionCalculates up to 20% deduction based on Schedule C profit
✅ Tax software handles this automatically You don't need to manually transfer numbers between forms. TurboTax, H&R Block, and TaxSlayer all auto-populate Schedule SE, Schedule 1, and Form 8995 based on your Schedule C entries. Understanding the flow just helps you sanity-check the numbers.
Section 8

Multiple Gigs — One Schedule C or Several?

This is one of the most common points of confusion. The rule: one Schedule C per distinct business activity, not one per platform.

Combine into ONE Schedule C if:

  • You drive for Uber AND Lyft AND DoorDash — all are "transportation/delivery services," one business
  • You freelance on Upwork AND Fiverr AND have direct clients — all the same type of work (e.g., web design)
  • The activities use the same skills, equipment, and business purpose

Use SEPARATE Schedule C forms if:

  • You drive for DoorDash AND also do freelance bookkeeping — genuinely different businesses
  • You sell on Etsy AND also do photography — different products/services, different expense categories
  • One business has employees or significant assets and the other doesn't
💡 When in doubt, combine similar work The IRS principal business code (entered at the top of Schedule C) should describe your overall activity. If Uber, Lyft, and DoorDash all fall under code 492000 (couriers and messengers) or 485300 (taxi/limousine service), they belong on one Schedule C. Separate distinctly different income streams.
Section 9

Common Schedule C Mistakes

Mistake 1: Reporting only 1099 amounts, missing cash/unreported income

Line 1 should include ALL business income — not just what appears on your 1099 forms. If you earned $300 cash from a client who didn't issue a 1099, it still belongs on Line 1.

Mistake 2: Mixing personal and business expenses

Only business-use expenses belong on Schedule C. If you deduct 100% of your phone bill but only use it 60% for work, you're over-deducting — and this is a common audit trigger.

Mistake 3: Forgetting Part IV vehicle questions

If you claim mileage on Line 9, you must answer the vehicle questions in Part IV truthfully, including whether you have written evidence. Skipping this section while claiming a large mileage deduction is a red flag.

Mistake 4: Double-deducting home office and actual home expenses

If you use the simplified home office method (Line 30), don't also separately deduct a portion of rent/utilities elsewhere on the form. Choose one method and apply it consistently.

Mistake 5: Not itemizing Part V "other expenses"

Lumping everything into a single "miscellaneous" line without itemization in Part V can raise questions in an audit. List each expense type separately, even if small.
