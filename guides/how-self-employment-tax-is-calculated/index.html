    How Self-Employment Tax Is Calculated

    Follow the major steps from business profit to Social Security and Medicare portions.

    Last reviewed: July 14, 2026

    Plain-language introduction

    Follow the major steps from business profit to Social Security and Medicare portions. This guide separates general planning concepts from facts and tax-year rules that need current official verification.

    Why this matters for 1099 workers

    The calculation contains several stages, and applying one percentage directly to gross receipts can materially distort a planning estimate.

    Step-by-step explanation

    1. Add gross receipts and other business income.
    2. Subtract supported business expenses to reach preliminary net profit.
    3. Apply the verified net-earnings percentage.
    4. Limit Social Security tax to the wage base remaining after applicable W-2 wages.
    5. Calculate Medicare without the Social Security wage-base cap.
    6. Review Additional Medicare thresholds and wages.
    7. Calculate the income-tax deduction associated with the employer-equivalent portion; it is not a cash refund.

    Key ideas

    • Apply the Schedule SE net-earnings methodology.
    • Limit the Social Security portion to the remaining annual wage base.
    • Review Additional Medicare rules separately.

    Common mistakes

    • Applying the combined rate directly to gross receipts
    • Ignoring W-2 wages already subject to Social Security tax
    • Using an old tax-year value or deadline without checking the current official source.

    What this guide does not cover

    How Self-Employment Tax Is Calculated focuses on its stated planning question. It does not calculate every credit, deduction, special tax, state or local rule, accounting method, entity rule, penalty exception, or eligibility limitation, and it does not prepare or file a return.

    When professional help may be appropriate

    Professional review may be useful when the how self-employment tax is calculated question involves multiple businesses, workers, inventory, depreciation, entity elections, multistate activity, notices, amended records, or facts outside the linked calculators.

    Records to gather

    Use invoices, payment statements, receipts, mileage or workspace records where relevant, prior returns, withholding statements, contracts, and estimated-payment confirmations.
