How Much Should You Set Aside for Taxes?

Every dollar you earn as a gig worker needs a tax-savings haircut taken off the top — but how much exactly? This guide gives you the precise percentage by income level, state, and situation so you're never caught off guard at tax time.

Section 1

The Quick Answer — 25-30% Rule

If you want the short answer right now: set aside 25% to 30% of every payment you receive from gig work or freelancing. Move it to a separate savings account immediately — ideally the same day you get paid.

The Default Rule for Most Gig Workers
25-30%
of every gross payment, set aside immediately

This single percentage covers your self-employment tax (15.3%) plus a reasonable estimate of federal income tax (10-15% for most earners after deductions). It's a starting point — the exact right number depends on your income level, state, and deductions, which we break down below.

✅ When in doubt, round up It's far better to set aside slightly too much and get a refund than to set aside too little and face a scramble in April. If unsure, save 30% rather than 25%.
Section 2

Why You Need to Set Money Aside at All

When you work a W-2 job, your employer automatically withholds taxes from every paycheck. You never see that money — it's gone before it hits your bank account, and by the time you file in April, you've usually already paid most or all of what you owe.

Gig platforms do the opposite: they pay you 100% of your earnings with zero withholding. Every dollar from Uber, DoorDash, or a freelance client lands in your account untaxed. If you spend it all, you'll face a real tax bill in April (or quarterly) with no money set aside to pay it.

⚠️ The most common gig worker tax mistake A first-year DoorDash driver earning $35,000 who spends every dollar as it comes in can face an $7,000-$8,000 tax bill they have zero savings to cover. This single mistake causes more financial stress for gig workers than almost anything else — and it's completely avoidable with one habit: set aside money the moment you get paid.

How to Calculate Your Exact Percentage

For a more precise number than the general rule of thumb, follow this process once at the start of the year (or whenever your income changes significantly):

📊 Quick Calculation Method

1. Estimate your annual net profitUse last year or current pace
2. Use our SE tax calculatorGet your exact tax estimate
3. Divide total tax by gross income= Your exact percentage
💰 Use that percentage going forwardApply to every payment
📋 Example: Finding your exact percentage
Estimated annual gross income$55,000
Estimated deductions (mileage, etc.)$10,000
Estimated net profit$45,000
SE Tax + Federal Tax (no state tax)$7,580
Your exact percentage (7,580 ÷ 55,000)13.8% of NET — but 25% of gross to be safe
💡 Why save based on gross, not the "real" percentage Notice in the example above, the actual tax owed is only 13.8% of gross — much lower than the 25% rule of thumb suggests. That's because deductions reduced the taxable base. But save based on the rule-of-thumb percentage anyway, applied to your GROSS payment. This builds in a safety margin — if your actual liability comes in lower (as it often does with good deductions), you end up with extra savings instead of a shortfall.

Section 6

Where to Actually Keep the Money

Having the right percentage doesn't help if the money isn't actually protected from being spent. Here are the best methods, ranked by effectiveness:

🏦

Separate High-Yield Savings Account

Best option. Open a dedicated savings account (Ally, Marcus, SoFi) purely for tax money. Earns 4-5% interest while it sits, and the separation from your checking account creates a psychological barrier against spending it.

🤖

Automatic Transfer Apps

Apps like Qapital or Digit can automatically move a percentage of incoming deposits to savings. Removes the willpower requirement entirely — the transfer happens before you even see the money.

📱

Gig-Specific Tax Apps

Apps like Keeper Tax or Hurdlr categorize income and automatically calculate suggested tax savings amounts, sometimes with built-in savings features.

💳

Separate Business Checking

For higher earners: open a dedicated business checking account. Route all gig income there first, then transfer your "take-home" portion to personal checking after setting aside the tax percentage.

🚨 What NOT to do Don't keep tax savings in the same checking account you spend from "in your head." Studies on financial behavior consistently show mental accounting fails under pressure — when an unexpected expense hits, that "earmarked" tax money becomes regular spending money in practice. Physical separation works; mental separation does not.
Section 7

How Deductions Change the Math

The percentages above are conservative estimates that assume modest deductions. If you're diligent about tracking mileage and other business expenses, your actual tax burden will likely be lower — meaning you might be able to save a smaller percentage, or simply end up with extra savings as a buffer.

Heavy mileage drivers (Uber, DoorDash, delivery)

If you drive significant miles, your mileage deduction (72.5¢/mile in 2026) can reduce your taxable net profit by 20-30%. A driver setting aside the standard 25-27% might find their actual liability closer to 18-20% — building a nice buffer or year-end refund.

Freelancers with high business expenses

Freelancers with significant equipment, software, and home office deductions may have actual tax rates several points lower than the rule-of-thumb percentage suggests. Still save the higher percentage — the extra becomes a tax refund or rolls into next year's safety cushion.

✅ The golden rule: never under-save based on assumed deductions Always calculate your set-aside percentage using conservative assumptions. It's far better to over-save and get money back than to under-save based on deductions you haven't actually tracked and documented yet.
Section 8

What If You've Already Fallen Behind?

If you're reading this mid-year and realize you haven't been setting money aside, here's how to course-correct:

Step 1: Calculate what you should have saved so far

Take your year-to-date gross income and multiply by your target percentage (25-35% depending on your situation). This is your target savings balance right now.

Step 2: Start saving the percentage from today forward

Don't try to "catch up" all at once — just start the habit immediately on all future income. Apply your full target percentage going forward.

Step 3: Make your next quarterly payment as large as possible

If a quarterly deadline is approaching, pay as much as you can toward your estimated tax bill, even if it's not the full amount. Partial payments reduce the underpayment penalty calculation compared to paying nothing.

Step 4: Consider a payment plan if needed

If April arrives and you genuinely cannot pay your full tax bill, file on time regardless (the failure-to-file penalty is much worse than failure-to-pay) and set up an IRS payment plan at IRS.gov/opa.
