The Basics

What Is the Home Office Deduction?

The home office deduction lets self-employed workers deduct the portion of their home expenses that relate to their business. If your home office takes up 10% of your home's square footage, you can deduct 10% of your rent (or mortgage interest), utilities, insurance, and other home costs as a business expense on Schedule C.

For gig workers, this deduction is often overlooked — but it's legitimate and valuable. If you spend time at home managing your DoorDash account, planning your Uber schedule, handling client invoices as a freelancer, or doing any other business-related work, you likely qualify.

✅ Key insight: The home office deduction isn't just for people with a dedicated room. It's for anyone who uses a defined space in their home regularly and exclusively for business. Even a dedicated desk area in a studio apartment can qualify — though a separate room is far easier to defend.

Who Can Claim It

To claim the home office deduction as a gig worker or freelancer, you must meet all three of these conditions:

  1. Regular use: You use the space for business on a regular basis — not just occasionally
  2. Exclusive use: The space is used only for business — no personal use of that specific area
  3. Principal place of business: Your home office is your main place of business, OR you use it to meet clients, OR it's a separate structure used for business
⚠️ The "exclusive use" rule is strict. If your home office doubles as a guest bedroom, kids' homework room, or personal hobby space — it does not qualify. The IRS is serious about this. The space must be used only for business, every time.
Do You Qualify?

Does Your Home Office Qualify? Real Scenarios

The regular and exclusive use rules trip up many gig workers. Here are common scenarios:

✅ These Qualify

  • Dedicated desk in a spare room used only for gig work admin
  • Separate room used only for freelance client calls and work
  • Detached garage converted to a home office or studio
  • Basement area clearly sectioned off and used only for business
  • Corner of a room with a dedicated desk used only for business — with clearly defined boundaries
  • Room used for client meetings that isn't used for anything else

❌ These Do NOT Qualify

  • Kitchen table where you also eat family meals
  • Living room couch where you occasionally check your delivery app
  • Bedroom desk where you also browse social media or watch TV
  • Guest bedroom used as office "most of the time" but guests stay there too
  • Shared workspace where family also uses the computer
  • Any space used even occasionally for personal purposes
📌 The "principal place of business" test for gig workers: Delivery drivers and rideshare workers do their actual gig work in their car — not at home. But if you use your home office to manage your business (accept orders, review earnings, do bookkeeping, track mileage), it still qualifies as your principal place of business for administrative purposes. The IRS allows this explicitly.

Method Comparison

Simplified Method vs. Regular Method

The IRS gives you two ways to calculate the home office deduction. You choose the method each year — unlike the mileage/actual vehicle expense choice, you can switch home office methods from year to year.

Simplified Method

$5 / sq ft

Up to 300 sq ft maximum ($1,500 cap)

  • No receipts or recordkeeping needed
  • Just measure your office space
  • Can switch to regular method in future years
  • No depreciation recapture when you sell your home
  • Deduction not limited by business income
  • Capped at $1,500 total ($5 × 300 sq ft)
  • May produce a smaller deduction than regular method
✅ Best for: Small offices, renters, and those who want simplicity

Regular Method

Actual % of Home Costs

Office sq ft ÷ home sq ft × all expenses

  • No dollar cap — larger deduction possible
  • Includes rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation
  • Better for large offices or high home expenses
  • Renters with high rent benefit the most
  • Requires tracking all home expenses with receipts
  • Must file Form 8829 with your return
  • Depreciation can trigger recapture tax when you sell
  • Deduction limited to net business income (no loss)
⚠️ Best for: Large offices, high rent/mortgage, or homeowners
✅ Quick rule of thumb: If your qualifying office is under 200 sq ft and your home expenses are moderate, the simplified method ($1,000 max at 200 sq ft) is usually fine. If your office is large or you pay high rent, run both calculations — the regular method can produce deductions 3–5× larger for people in expensive housing markets.
Regular Method Details

What Home Expenses Can You Deduct? (Regular Method)

Under the regular method, you calculate your business-use percentage (office sq ft ÷ total home sq ft) and apply it to your home expenses. Some expenses apply only to the office (direct) while others apply to the whole home (indirect).

⚠️ Homeowners: be cautious about depreciation. When you sell your home, any depreciation you claimed on the home office portion is subject to "depreciation recapture" tax — you'll owe tax on that amount even if the home qualifies for the principal residence exclusion. Renters don't face this issue. For homeowners with a small office, the simplified method often makes more sense for this reason alone.
Filing Your Taxes

How to Claim the Home Office Deduction on Your Return

1

Measure your office space

Measure the square footage of the area used exclusively for business. For a dedicated room, measure length × width. For a defined area within a room, measure only that area. Take a photo for your records showing the clear boundaries of the space.

2

Choose your method

Decide between the simplified method ($5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft) or the regular method (actual expenses × business use %). If using the regular method, total all your home expenses for the year from receipts and statements.

3

Simplified: enter on Schedule C directly

If using the simplified method, calculate your deduction (sq ft × $5, max $1,500) and enter it on Schedule C, Part II, Line 30. No additional form required.

4

Regular method: complete Form 8829

If using the regular method, complete Form 8829 (Expenses for Business Use of Your Home). This form calculates your business use percentage and applies it to each category of home expense. The result flows to Schedule C Line 30. Tax software handles this automatically.

5

Keep records in case of audit

Save photos of your dedicated office space, floor plans or measurements, and expense receipts for 3 years. You don't submit these — but if the IRS audits you, the exclusive-use requirement is the most scrutinized part of this deduction.

📌 Important limit: Under the regular method, your home office deduction cannot exceed your net business income for the year. If your gig work lost money or broke even, you can't use the home office deduction to create a loss (though you can carry the unused deduction to the next year). The simplified method has no income limitation.

Document when you started using the space for business — even a calendar entry or lease date helps establish the timeline.
Yes — office furniture and equipment are separate deductions from the home office deduction itself. Under the regular method, furniture and equipment used exclusively in the office are 100% deductible direct expenses (or via Section 179 immediate expensing). The $5/sq ft simplified rate does not include furniture — it covers only the structural home costs like rent and utilities.
