Top 20 Tax Deductions for Gig Workers & Freelancers

You only pay taxes on your net profit — not your gross income. Every deduction on this list reduces what the IRS taxes you on. Here are all 20, explained fully with amounts, who qualifies, and how to claim each one.

Unlike W-2 employees who have very limited deductions, gig workers and freelancers can deduct a wide range of real business expenses directly from their gross income. This reduces your net profit — the base for self-employment tax (15.3%) and income tax. Every dollar deducted saves you roughly 27-40 cents in combined taxes depending on your bracket.

All deductions below are reported on Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business) unless noted otherwise. Use our SE tax calculator to see how your deductions reduce your actual tax bill.

🚗 Vehicle & Transportation
1
🚗
Mileage Deduction
Your single largest deduction as a driver
72.5¢ per mile
Schedule C, Line 9

For every business mile you drive in 2026, you deduct 72.5 cents from your gross income. This covers gas, depreciation, insurance, and maintenance in one simple rate — no receipt tracking required, just a mileage log.

Example: 18,000 business miles × 72.5¢ = $13,050 deduction. At a combined 30% tax rate, that saves $3,915 in taxes.

💡 Track tip: Use Stride (free) or MileIQ ($5.99/mo) to auto-track every mile via GPS. DoorDash and Uber only report "active" miles — a separate app captures 25-35% more deductible miles the platform misses.
Uber/Lyft drivers DoorDash drivers Instacart shoppers Amazon Flex Freelancers who drive to clients
2
⛽
Actual Vehicle Expenses
Alternative to mileage — track real costs instead
Business % of total costs
Schedule C, Line 9

Instead of the standard mileage rate, you can deduct your actual vehicle operating costs multiplied by your business-use percentage. Includes gas, insurance, oil changes, repairs, tires, registration, and depreciation.

Example: $8,000 total vehicle costs × 70% business use = $5,600 deduction. You must choose between standard mileage and actual expenses at the start of the year — you can't switch mid-year.

💡 When to use this method: Only beneficial for low-mileage drivers with expensive vehicles or high maintenance costs. For most high-mileage gig drivers, the standard mileage rate wins.
All drivers Best for low-mileage, high-cost vehicles
3
🅿️
Parking Fees & Tolls
100% deductible — even when using standard mileage
100% deductible
Schedule C, Line 9 or 27a

All parking fees and tolls paid while working are 100% deductible — and unlike most vehicle costs, you can deduct these even if you're also using the standard mileage rate. NYC drivers especially: bridge and tunnel tolls add up to thousands per year.

💡 Log tolls in your mileage app as they happen. EZPass and toll receipt apps make this easy to document at year end.
All drivers Deductible even with standard mileage rate
🏠 Home & Office
4
🏠
Home Office Deduction
Dedicated workspace used exclusively for work
Up to $1,500/year
Schedule C, Line 30 / Form 8829

If you have a dedicated area of your home used exclusively and regularly for business, you can deduct it. The simplified method: $5 per square foot, maximum 300 sq ft = up to $1,500/year. No receipt tracking — just measure the space.

Example: A 200 sq ft dedicated office = 200 × $5 = $1,000 deduction. Saves $153 in SE tax and additional income tax savings on top.

💡 "Exclusively" is the key word — a corner of your bedroom where you also watch TV doesn't qualify. A desk in a room you only use for work does. Take a photo for documentation.
Freelancers Remote workers Any gig worker with dedicated workspace
📱 Technology & Equipment
5
📱
Phone & Data Plan
Business percentage of your monthly bill
50–80% of annual bill
Schedule C, Line 25

If you use your phone for gig work, deduct the business-use percentage of your monthly bill. Drivers who use their phone primarily for navigation and apps can typically deduct 70-80%. Freelancers who also use it personally may deduct 50-60%.

Example: $80/month bill × 75% business use × 12 months = $720 deduction.

💡 Keep a simple note of what percentage of your phone usage is business vs. personal. The IRS may ask you to substantiate this in an audit.
All gig workers All freelancers
6
💻
Equipment & Hardware (Section 179)
Deduct the full cost in the year of purchase
Full cost, year of purchase
Schedule C, Line 13

Business equipment — laptop, monitors, webcam, microphone, camera, dash cam, tablet — can be fully deducted in the year you buy it using Section 179 expensing. No depreciation schedule needed for most equipment under $1 million.

Example: Buy a $1,800 laptop for freelance work in March 2026 → deduct the full $1,800 on your 2026 return. Saves $275 in SE tax plus income tax savings.

💡 Keep the purchase receipt and note what % of use is business. A laptop used 80% for work = deduct 80% of the cost if you also use it personally.
Freelancers Content creators Drivers (dash cam, phone mount)
7
🔧
Software & Subscriptions
Every work-related app and subscription
100% of business subscriptions
Schedule C, Line 18 or 27a

Any software subscription used for your business is fully deductible. Adobe Creative Cloud, Figma, GitHub, Notion, Slack, Zoom, project management tools, mileage tracking apps, accounting software — all 100% deductible.

💡 Review your credit card statements for all recurring software charges you may have forgotten about. Many freelancers miss $500-$2,000/year in software deductions.
All freelancers All gig workers with work apps

💊 Health & Retirement — The Big Two
8
💊
Health Insurance Premiums
100% deductible if self-employed — not on Schedule C
100% of premiums
Schedule 1, Line 17 (above-the-line)

If gig work or freelancing is your primary income source and you pay for your own health insurance, you can deduct 100% of premiums for yourself, your spouse, and your dependents. This is an above-the-line deduction — it reduces your gross income before SE tax and before the standard deduction.

Example: $600/month health insurance premium = $7,200/year deduction. Saves $1,101 in SE tax plus significant income tax savings.

💡 This deduction does NOT go on Schedule C. It goes on Schedule 1 Line 17 (Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction). Tax software places it automatically when you indicate you're self-employed.
Primary income is self-employment Not eligible through employer plan
9
🏦
SEP-IRA Contributions
The single largest tax reduction available to gig workers
Up to $70,000/year
Schedule 1, Line 16

A SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension) lets you contribute up to 25% of your net self-employment income, maximum $70,000 in 2026. Every dollar contributed is fully deductible from your gross income — reducing both income tax AND self-employment tax base.

Example: $20,000 SEP-IRA contribution saves approximately $3,060 in SE tax + $4,400 in federal income tax = $7,460 total tax savings. The money grows tax-deferred until retirement.

💡 Open a SEP-IRA at Fidelity, Vanguard, or Schwab in about 20 minutes. You have until the tax filing deadline (including extensions) to contribute for the prior year. This is the most powerful single tax lever available to gig workers.
All self-employed workers Best benefit for higher earners
10
💰
Solo 401(k) Contributions
Higher limits than SEP-IRA for some situations
Up to $70,000/year
Schedule 1, Line 16

A Solo 401(k) (also called Individual 401(k)) allows both employee and employer contributions, with a combined limit of $70,000 in 2026. You can contribute more at lower income levels than a SEP-IRA because the employee contribution ($23,500) doesn't depend on your profit percentage.

Best for: Freelancers earning $50,000-$100,000 who want to maximize retirement contributions and tax deductions. Must be established by December 31 of the tax year (unlike SEP-IRA which can be opened up to the filing deadline).

No full-time employees Higher contribution potential at mid-range incomes
📋 Tax-Related Deductions
11
⚖️
½ Self-Employment Tax Deduction
Automatic deduction — reduces your income tax
50% of SE tax paid
Schedule 1, Line 15

The IRS allows you to deduct exactly 50% of your self-employment tax from your gross income before calculating income tax. This partially compensates for the fact that employees' employers pay half their FICA taxes.

Example: $8,000 SE tax → deduct $4,000 from gross income. If you're in the 22% bracket, that saves $880 in income tax. This happens automatically on Schedule 1 — tax software handles it.

All self-employed workers automatically
12
📉
Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction
Deduct up to 20% of net business income
Up to 20% of net income
Form 8995, flows to Schedule 1

Most gig workers and freelancers can deduct up to 20% of their qualified business income (net profit minus the QBI deduction itself). This is one of the most commonly overlooked deductions. It directly reduces your federal income tax bill, though it doesn't reduce SE tax.

Example: $50,000 net profit × 20% = $10,000 QBI deduction. If you're in the 22% bracket, that saves $2,200 in income tax.

💡 This deduction phases out for high earners (above $197,300 single, $394,600 married for 2026) and certain service businesses. Tax software calculates this automatically on Form 8995.
Most gig workers Most freelancers Sole proprietors
13
💾
Tax Preparation Costs
Tax software and CPA fees for your business return
100% of business portion
Schedule C, Line 17

The portion of your tax preparation costs related to your business return is deductible. TurboTax Self-Employed, H&R Block Self-Employed, CPA fees — the business-related portion (typically most of it for gig workers) goes on Schedule C.

All self-employed filers

Delivery Supplies & Equipment
Hot bags, coolers, packaging — everything you use for deliveries
100% deductible
Schedule C, Line 22

Insulated delivery bags, pizza bags, grocery totes, coolers, food warming bags — any equipment you use specifically for deliveries is 100% deductible. Also includes packaging materials, labels, or any supply specific to your delivery work.

DoorDash drivers Instacart shoppers Amazon Flex drivers All delivery workers

🌐
Internet Service (Business %)
Home internet used for work — deduct the business portion
Business % of monthly bill
Schedule C, Line 25

If you use home internet for business (uploading work, client communication, platform apps, research), deduct the business-use percentage. Freelancers working from home: 50-80%. Gig workers who only use cellular: typically covered by the phone deduction instead.

Freelancers working from home Remote workers
16
🎓
Education & Professional Development
Courses and training that improve skills in your current work
100% if work-related
Schedule C, Line 27a

Any education that helps you maintain or improve skills in your current field is deductible. Online courses (Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning), books, industry conferences, professional certifications — all deductible.

Important limitation: Cannot be for training in a new career or field. A DoorDash driver taking a cooking class isn't deductible; a freelance developer taking an advanced React course is.

All freelancers Professional development in your current field
17
🏢
Platform Service Fees
Upwork, Fiverr, and other platform commissions
100% deductible
Schedule C, Line 27a

Fees charged by freelancing platforms are deductible business expenses. Upwork's 5-20% service fee, Fiverr's 20% commission, Toptal's margin — all deductible. Important: if your 1099 shows gross earnings before the fee, deduct the fee separately on Schedule C Part V.

Upwork freelancers Fiverr sellers All platform workers
18
🚿
Car Cleaning & Detailing
Business percentage of car cleaning costs
Business % deductible
Schedule C, Line 27a

Rideshare and delivery drivers who must maintain a clean vehicle for passengers or professional image can deduct the business-use percentage of car washing and detailing costs. Keep receipts.

Uber/Lyft drivers Delivery drivers

🍬
Rider & Customer Supplies
Water, mints, chargers — anything you provide to riders
100% deductible
Schedule C, Line 22

Water bottles, mints, phone charging cables, air fresheners, hand sanitizer — anything you purchase to provide to riders or customers as part of your service. Keep receipts and a note of business purpose.

Uber/Lyft drivers Any service-focused driver
20
🏥
HSA Contributions (Health Savings Account)
Triple tax advantage for high-deductible health plan holders
$4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2026)
Schedule 1, Line 13

If you have a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), you can contribute to a Health Savings Account (HSA) and deduct every dollar contributed. HSAs have a triple tax advantage: contributions are deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free.

2026 HSA limits: $4,300 for individual coverage, $8,550 for family coverage. Contributions can be made until tax filing deadline.

HDHP health plan holders only Great complement to freelancer health insurance
💡 Combined savings potential A full-time gig worker who tracks all applicable deductions — mileage, phone, home office, health insurance, and SEP-IRA — can reduce their taxable net profit by $25,000-$40,000+ compared to claiming no deductions. At a 30% combined tax rate, that's $7,500-$12,000 in annual tax savings.
