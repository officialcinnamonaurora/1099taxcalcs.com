Section 1

What Are Quarterly Estimated Taxes?

Quarterly estimated taxes are advance payments toward your annual tax bill, made four times per year instead of all at once in April. The US tax system is "pay as you go" — the IRS expects you to pay taxes as you earn income throughout the year, not in one lump sum after the fact.

W-2 employees satisfy this automatically through paycheck withholding. Self-employed workers — gig drivers, freelancers, independent contractors — have no employer withholding anything, so the IRS requires them to estimate and pay quarterly instead.

💡 Think of it as self-withholding Quarterly payments are exactly what your employer would have withheld if you had a W-2 job — except you calculate and send it yourself, four times per year, instead of it happening automatically every paycheck.
Section 2

Who Must Pay Quarterly Taxes?

You're required to make quarterly estimated payments if both of these are true:

  • You expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax for the year after subtracting withholding and credits
  • Your withholding (if any, from a W-2 job) won't cover at least 90% of your tax bill

In practice, almost every full-time gig worker, freelancer, and independent contractor meets this threshold. Even a DoorDash driver earning $15,000/year in net profit will typically owe more than $1,000 in combined SE tax and income tax.

You probably need to pay quarterly if:

  • Gig work or freelancing is your primary income source
  • You have significant side income on top of a W-2 job (over $7,000/year net)
  • You're a sole proprietor or single-member LLC owner
  • You receive rental income, investment income, or other untaxed income streams

You might NOT need to pay quarterly if:

  • Your total tax liability is under $1,000 for the year
  • You had zero tax liability the previous full year (and were a US citizen for the whole year)
  • Your W-2 withholding alone covers 90%+ of your total tax bill

⚠️ Q2 is only 2 months, not 3 This trips up many gig workers. The June 16 deadline covers only April and May income — not June. June income rolls into the Q3 (September) payment instead. The four periods are: 3 months, 2 months, 3 months, 4 months — not equal quarters.

What if a deadline falls on a weekend or holiday?

The deadline automatically shifts to the next business day. June 15, 2026 falls on a Sunday, which is why the actual deadline is June 16. Always double-check the exact date each year at IRS.gov.

Alternative to the Q4 payment

You can skip the January 15 Q4 payment if you file your complete tax return and pay any remaining balance in full by January 31, 2027. This works well if you have your full-year numbers finalized early and want to avoid an extra payment step.

How to Calculate Your Quarterly Payment

There are two main approaches: estimate based on current year income, or use last year's tax bill (safe harbor). Here's the current-year estimation method:

// STEP 1: Estimate annual net profit
Annual Net Profit = Expected Gross Income − Expected Deductions

// STEP 2: Calculate annual SE tax
SE Tax = Net Profit × 0.9235 × 0.153

// STEP 3: Calculate annual income tax
Income Tax = brackets(Net Profit − ½ SE Tax − Standard Deduction)

// STEP 4: Add state tax if applicable
Total Annual Tax = SE Tax + Income Tax + State Tax

// STEP 5: Divide by 4
Quarterly Payment = Total Annual Tax ÷ 4

Section 5

The Safe Harbor Rule — Guaranteed No Penalty

If estimating your current year income feels stressful or uncertain, the IRS offers a simpler, guaranteed-safe alternative: pay based on last year's tax bill instead.

Safe Harbor RuleWho It Applies ToWhat You Pay
90% Current Year RuleEveryone90% of this year's actual final tax liability
100% Prior Year RuleAGI $150,000 or less last year100% of last year's total tax bill, split into 4 payments
110% Prior Year RuleAGI over $150,000 last year110% of last year's total tax bill, split into 4 payments
✅ The easiest path for most gig workers Find your 2025 Form 1040, Line 24 (Total Tax). Divide by 4. Pay that exact amount each quarter in 2026. You are now 100% penalty-proof for the entire year — even if your income doubles. This is the simplest, most stress-free approach and is what most tax professionals recommend for variable-income workers.

Why the safe harbor rule is so powerful

Imagine you had a breakout year and your income jumped from $50,000 to $120,000. If you pay based on last year's (lower) tax bill, you meet safe harbor and owe zero penalty — even though you'll owe a large balance when you file in April. You just need to have the cash ready to pay that balance by April 15.

Section 6

How to Pay Quarterly Taxes — Step by Step

1

Go to IRS.gov/payments

Click "Make a Payment." Select "Estimated Tax" as the reason for payment, and select tax year 2026. No account creation or login is required for this method.

2

Verify your identity

Enter your name, SSN, date of birth, and address. The system cross-checks against a prior-year tax return for verification — have your 2025 return nearby in case you need details.

3

Enter your payment amount and date

Type in your quarterly amount (use our quarterly tax calculator if unsure). Choose your payment date — schedule it for on or before the deadline.

4

Choose ACH bank transfer (free)

IRS Direct Pay via bank account (ACH) is completely free. Credit/debit card payment is available through third-party processors but charges a 1.87–2.49% fee — only worth it for credit card rewards points.

5

Save your confirmation number

Screenshot or print the confirmation. At tax time, your tax software will ask you to enter all quarterly payments made — this gets credited against your final bill on Form 1040, Line 26.

Alternative payment methods

  • IRS2Go app: Official IRS mobile app, free, available on iOS and Android — pay in under 2 minutes
  • EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System): Requires advance enrollment (5-7 business days) but allows scheduling all 4 payments in advance
  • Check by mail: Mail Form 1040-ES with a check — slowest method, not recommended unless necessary
Section 7

What If Your Income Is Uneven?

Many gig workers and freelancers don't earn the same amount every quarter — holiday season delivery work spikes, freelance projects cluster unpredictably, seasonal businesses have natural highs and lows.

Option 1: Pay equal quarterly amounts (simplest)

Estimate your full-year tax bill and divide by 4, regardless of which quarter the income actually arrives in. This is the simplest approach and works fine for the safe harbor rule.

Option 2: Annualized Income Installment Method

If your income is heavily weighted toward certain quarters (e.g., a freelance graphic designer who earns 70% of annual income in Q4 holiday season), you can use IRS Form 2210, Schedule AI to calculate each payment based on actual income earned in that specific quarter rather than equal installments.

This more complex method can reduce or eliminate penalties if you genuinely earn very little in Q1–Q2 and most of your income in Q3–Q4. It requires more detailed tracking but can save you from overpaying early in the year when cash flow is tight.

💡 When to use the annualized method Only worth the extra complexity if your income swings dramatically by quarter (more than 2x difference between your lowest and highest quarter). For most gig workers with relatively steady income, equal quarterly payments are simpler and sufficient.
Section 8

The Underpayment Penalty Explained

If you don't meet a safe harbor and underpay your quarterly taxes, the IRS charges an underpayment penalty — essentially interest on the amount you should have paid but didn't.

⚠️ How the penalty is calculated

2026 underpayment interest rate~8% annually
Calculated per quarter underpaidFrom due date until paid
Example: $2,000 underpaid for 1 full quarter~$40 penalty
Example: $5,000 underpaid for 2 quarters~$200 penalty

The penalty is not catastrophic — it's roughly equivalent to a high-yield savings account interest rate. But it's still money you don't need to pay if you plan ahead. The penalty is calculated on IRS Form 2210, which most tax software completes automatically if you indicate you didn't make full quarterly payments.

When the IRS waives the penalty automatically

  • Your total tax liability after withholding is under $1,000
  • You had zero tax liability for the full prior year
  • You're a farmer or fisherman (special rules apply)
  • You can show the underpayment was due to casualty, disaster, or other unusual circumstance
Section 9

Common Quarterly Tax Mistakes

Mistake 1: Waiting until April to think about taxes

By the time you file your annual return, three of the four quarterly deadlines have already passed. If you've made zero payments by April, you've already accumulated underpayment penalties for Q1, Q2, and Q3 — even though you're paying everything "on time" with your April filing.

Mistake 2: Only paying what you can "afford" each quarter

If cash is tight and you pay less than required, you're accumulating both a tax debt AND a penalty. It's almost always better to reduce spending elsewhere to make the full quarterly payment, since the IRS underpayment rate (8%) is higher than most people earn on savings.

Mistake 3: Forgetting state quarterly taxes

Many states with income tax (California, New York, Oregon, and others) have their own separate quarterly estimated tax requirements with different deadlines and forms. Federal payment does not cover state tax. Check your state's tax agency website.

Mistake 4: Not adjusting after a big income change

If you land a major new client or your gig income suddenly doubles, recalculate your remaining quarterly payments. Sticking to your original (now too-low) estimate for the rest of the year can leave you with a large penalty and a big April surprise.

Mistake 5: Confusing extension with extra time to pay

Filing an extension (Form 4868) gives you until October 15 to file your paperwork — but any tax owed is still due April 15. An extension does not extend your payment deadline, and interest accrues from April 15 regardless of when you actually file.
