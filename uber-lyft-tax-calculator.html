Rideshare & Taxes

How Uber & Lyft Taxes Actually Work

Both Uber and Lyft classify all drivers as independent contractors. This means neither platform withholds a single dollar from your earnings for taxes. You receive 100% of your pay and are completely responsible for calculating and paying your own taxes.

This surprises many new drivers who come from traditional employment where taxes are automatically withheld. As a rideshare driver, you owe two separate taxes: self-employment tax and income tax — and you must pay them quarterly to avoid IRS penalties.

The Big Mileage Secret

Deadhead Miles — The Deduction Uber Doesn't Track

The single biggest tax mistake rideshare drivers make is only deducting the miles Uber or Lyft reports. The platform only tracks miles during active trips. You can deduct far more.

✅

Fully Deductible

Miles during active passenger rides · Miles driving to pick up a passenger · Miles between trips while online · Miles to gas station for work · Miles to car wash for work

⚠️

Partially Deductible

Miles from home to first pickup (disputed — log them anyway) · Miles after last drop-off heading home · Miles to buy supplies or accessories for driving

❌

Not Deductible

Personal errands during a work day · Vacation miles · Miles while the app is completely off · Commuting to a separate regular W-2 job

⚠️ The 30% rule: Most full-time Uber and Lyft drivers find their total deductible miles are 25–35% higher than what the app reports. On 17,000 reported miles, your actual deductible miles are likely 21,000–23,000. That's an extra $2,900–$4,350 in deductions — saving roughly $800–$1,200 in taxes. Use Stride (free) to capture every mile automatically.
