1099-NEC vs 1099-K — What's the Difference?

Got a 1099-NEC, a 1099-K, or both — and not sure what to do? This complete 2026 guide explains exactly what each form is, who sends them, what changed with the new $600 threshold, and how to report each one on your tax return.

Section 2

What Is Form 1099-NEC?

The 1099-NEC (Nonemployee Compensation) is the primary tax form for independent contractors and gig workers. The IRS reinstated it in 2020 after previously using Box 7 of the 1099-MISC for this purpose.

A business or platform sends you a 1099-NEC when they paid you $600 or more during the year for services as a nonemployee. "Services" includes driving for Uber, delivering for DoorDash, completing projects on Upwork — essentially any work you did as an independent contractor rather than an employee.

What's reported on a 1099-NEC

  • Box 1 — Nonemployee Compensation: The total amount the business paid you. This is the number you report on Schedule C.
  • Box 4 — Federal income tax withheld: Usually $0 for gig workers. If backup withholding applies (rare), it appears here.
  • Boxes 5–7 — State information: State income reported and any state taxes withheld.

Who sends a 1099-NEC to gig workers

  • Uber and Lyft — for incentive and bonus payments
  • DoorDash — for base Dasher pay and promotion payments
  • Instacart — for shopper pay
  • Amazon Flex — for delivery payments
  • Upwork — for contract earnings reported to IRS
  • Direct clients — if they paid you $600+ as a contractor
💡 The $600 threshold is for the sender, not for you The platform only has to send a 1099-NEC if they paid you $600 or more. But you must report ALL self-employment income over $400 net — even if you earned $350 from a client and got no form. The form is for the platform's reporting obligation; your reporting obligation has a different (lower) threshold.
Section 3

What Is Form 1099-K?

The 1099-K (Payment Card and Third Party Network Transactions) reports payments made through third-party payment processors. It was originally designed for merchants accepting credit cards, but has expanded significantly to cover gig economy payments and digital wallets.

A 1099-K is sent by the payment processor — not necessarily the company you worked for. If DoorDash processes customer tips through their payment system and passes them to you, DoorDash (as the payment processor) may send you a 1099-K for those tips. Meanwhile, DoorDash may also send you a 1099-NEC for base pay.

Who sends a 1099-K

  • Uber/Lyft — for trip fare payments processed through the app
  • DoorDash/Grubhub — for customer-paid tips and some earnings
  • PayPal/Venmo — if you received $600+ for goods or services
  • Stripe/Square — for any business payments processed
  • Cash App (Business) — for business account transactions
  • Etsy/eBay — for seller payments processed through their systems
⚠️ Personal transfers do NOT belong on 1099-K If your friend sends you $700 via Venmo to split rent or reimburse you for dinner, that is NOT income and does not belong on your tax return. The IRS knows personal transfers happen — mark payments as "personal" in PayPal/Venmo to prevent them from appearing on a 1099-K. If you receive an incorrect 1099-K including personal payments, you can report only the business income portion on Schedule C and document the discrepancy.

What this means for gig workers in 2026

The $600 threshold means many more gig workers will receive a 1099-K for the first time in early 2027 (covering 2026 earnings). If you earned $600 or more through PayPal, Venmo (business account), Stripe, or any gig platform's payment processing system, you will receive a 1099-K.

🚨 This is NOT new income — it's newly reported income The $600 threshold change does not create a new tax. You owe the same SE tax and income tax on gig earnings regardless of the threshold. What changed is that platforms are now required to report it to the IRS. If you've been under-reporting gig income, the IRS will now have records it didn't before.

Common confusion: Am I taxed differently because of 1099-K?

No. Whether your income appears on a 1099-NEC, a 1099-K, or no form at all, it's all reported the same way — on Schedule C — and taxed the same way. The form is just an information return that helps the IRS cross-check your reporting. It doesn't change your tax rate or liability.

Section 6

What If You Receive Both Forms From the Same Platform?

This is one of the most confusing situations gig workers face — receiving both a 1099-NEC and a 1099-K from the same company like Uber or DoorDash. Many people worry they're being taxed twice. They're not.

Here's what's happening: the platform is reporting different types of payments on different forms:

  • 1099-NEC: Direct payments from the company to you — base pay, bonuses, incentives
  • 1099-K: Payments processed through their payment system — often customer tips, or trip fares processed as card transactions
📋 Example: Uber Driver Receives Both Forms

Maria drives for Uber full-time and earns $52,000 for the year. She receives:

1099-NEC: $4,200 — Quest bonuses, referral bonuses, and surge pay incentives paid directly by Uber

1099-K: $47,800 — Trip fares and tips processed through the Uber payment system

Total income: $52,000 — She reports the combined $52,000 on Schedule C. She does NOT add $4,200 + $47,800 = $52,000 and then add the forms together — she just uses the total. The two forms together account for all her income, not twice her income.

How to avoid double-counting

1

Add up both form amounts

Take all 1099-NEC Box 1 amounts and all 1099-K Box 1a amounts from the same platform. Add them together.

2

Compare to your actual earnings

Check your platform's annual earnings summary or payment history. The total should match (or be close to) the sum of both forms. If they match — great. If not, flag the discrepancy.

3

Report the actual total on Schedule C

Report your actual total income earned — not just the form amounts. If forms total $52,000 and your earnings summary also shows $52,000, report $52,000 on Schedule C.

Section 7

What If You Didn't Receive Any 1099 Form?

Not receiving a 1099 form does not mean your income is tax-free. The form is the platform's reporting obligation — your reporting obligation is separate and has a lower threshold.

You still must report income if:

  • Your net self-employment earnings exceeded $400 (regardless of forms received)
  • A client paid you less than $600 (below their 1099-NEC threshold)
  • You were paid in cash with no records sent to the IRS
  • You earned income from a new platform that doesn't have your tax information yet

Reasons you might not receive a 1099

  • You earned less than $600 from that specific payer (below their reporting threshold)
  • The platform has an incorrect address or SSN on file — update it immediately
  • The form was sent to a wrong address — check your email and platform tax center first
  • The platform is late sending it (must be postmarked by January 31)
⚠️ Don't wait for a form that might not come If January 31 passes and you haven't received your expected 1099 forms, contact the platform directly. For missing or incorrect 1099s from a business client, contact the payer. If you still can't get it by mid-February, contact the IRS (1-800-829-1040) and they can send a substitute form letter. Report your income using your own records — don't skip it just because the form didn't arrive.
Section 8

How to Report Each Form on Your Taxes

Both forms ultimately end up in the same place: Schedule C (Profit or Loss from Business) attached to your Form 1040. Here's the step-by-step reporting process:

1

Gather all your 1099 forms

Download every 1099-NEC and 1099-K from all platforms. Also gather your own earnings records — platform summaries, bank statements, mileage logs — to verify accuracy.

2

Complete Schedule C for your business

Enter your total gross income on Line 1 of Schedule C. This is the combined total from all 1099-NEC Box 1 amounts, all 1099-K Box 1a amounts, and any cash income not reflected in forms. Then list all business deductions (Part II of Schedule C) — mileage, home office, equipment, etc.

3

Schedule SE calculates your SE tax

The net profit from Schedule C flows to Schedule SE, which calculates your 15.3% SE tax on 92.35% of net profit. Tax software does this automatically.

4

Both schedules attach to Form 1040

Your completed Schedule C and Schedule SE attach to your Form 1040. The SE tax flows to Form 1040 Schedule 2, and the ½ SE tax deduction flows to Schedule 1. Tax software handles all of this automatically.

✅ Let tax software handle it TurboTax Self-Employed, H&R Block Self-Employed, and TaxSlayer Self-Employed all handle 1099-NEC and 1099-K reporting correctly. Many platforms allow you to import your tax forms directly into TurboTax, saving manual entry time. The software asks the right questions and routes everything to the correct form.

Section 9

Common Mistakes With 1099 Forms

Mistake 1: Double-counting income from both forms

Receiving a 1099-NEC for $4,000 and a 1099-K for $48,000 does not mean you earned $52,000 — unless those are truly separate income streams. If they're from the same platform reporting different aspects of the same earnings, check your annual summary to find your actual total.

Mistake 2: Not reporting income below the form threshold

If you earned $450 from a client and got no 1099-NEC (below $600), you still must report that $450 on Schedule C. The IRS requires reporting all self-employment income over $400 net — the $600 threshold is for the payer, not for you.

Mistake 3: Including personal transfers in 1099-K income

If your 1099-K from PayPal includes $3,000 in personal friend reimbursements alongside $8,000 in actual business income, you only report the $8,000 as income. Keep detailed records. If the form is incorrect, you can report the correct amount on Schedule C and attach an explanation — or request a corrected 1099-K from the payer.

Mistake 4: Forgetting to deduct the platform's service fee

Upwork, Fiverr, and other platforms report your gross contract value on your 1099 — before their fee. Their fee is a deductible business expense. If Upwork shows $100,000 on your 1099 but took a $10,000 fee, you deduct $10,000 as a business expense on Schedule C, leaving $90,000 net income. Don't pay taxes on money you never received.

Mistake 5: Not reconciling form amounts with actual earnings

Always compare your 1099 form amounts to your actual earnings records. Platforms make mistakes. If a form overstates your income, contact the platform for a corrected 1099 before filing — or file with your actual correct amount and document the discrepancy.
