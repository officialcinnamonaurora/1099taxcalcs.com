The Basics

What Is the Mileage Deduction and How Does It Work?

The IRS allows self-employed individuals and gig workers to deduct vehicle costs one of two ways: the standard mileage rate or the actual expense method. The standard mileage method is the simpler approach — you multiply your business miles by the IRS rate and deduct the result.

For 2026, every business mile you drive is worth 72.5 cents as a tax deduction. That deduction reduces your net profit on Schedule C, which lowers both your income tax and your 15.3% self-employment tax. The mileage deduction is often the single largest deduction available to delivery drivers and rideshare workers.

📌 Example: You drove 18,000 business miles for DoorDash in 2026. Your mileage deduction = 18,000 × $0.725 = $13,050. If your gross DoorDash earnings were $28,000, your taxable net profit drops to $14,950. That deduction alone could save you over $3,000 in combined income and SE taxes.

Where to Claim It on Your Tax Return

Report your mileage deduction on Schedule C, Part II, Line 9 (Car and Truck Expenses). You'll also need to complete Part IV of Schedule C, which asks questions about your vehicle (date placed in service, total miles, business miles, commuting miles, other miles). Tax software walks you through all of this automatically.

What Counts

Which Miles Are Deductible for Gig Workers?

Not every mile you drive is a business mile. The IRS has specific rules — and gig platforms add another layer of complexity. Here's the breakdown:

✅ Miles That ARE Deductible

  • Driving while on an active delivery or ride request — from the moment you accept a job to when it's complete
  • Driving between deliveries — the deadhead miles from your last dropoff to your next pickup are deductible
  • Driving to/from a customer or client location — freelancers meeting clients, photographers at shoots, etc.
  • Driving to buy business supplies — picking up supplies, equipment, or materials for your gig business
  • Driving to the bank, post office, or other business errands — when the errand is for your business
  • Driving to a second work location from your first — if you have multiple gig jobs in a day

❌ Miles That Are NOT Deductible

  • Commuting from home to a fixed workplace — driving to a regular office or warehouse where you work daily doesn't count
  • Personal driving — grocery runs, dropping kids at school, personal errands
  • Driving while not on a platform or active for business — if the app is closed, the miles don't count
  • Driving to and from a permanent office you own or rent — your "business location" commute is not deductible
⚠️ The "online but waiting" question: Uber and Lyft drivers often ask whether miles driven while online but waiting for a request count. The IRS has not issued definitive guidance specifically on this. Many tax professionals advise deducting only miles with an accepted request (pickup to dropoff). To be safe, track all online miles but consult a CPA about your specific situation.

Method Comparison

Standard Mileage vs. Actual Expense Method

You have two choices for deducting vehicle costs. You must pick one method per vehicle per year — and if you use the actual expense method in Year 1, you generally can't switch to standard mileage later.

Standard Mileage Rate

72.5¢ per business mile × total miles

  • Simple — just track your miles
  • No need to save gas receipts, insurance docs, etc.
  • Can still deduct parking fees and tolls separately
  • Usually better for high-mileage, fuel-efficient cars
  • Can switch to actual expense method in future years
  • Can't deduct depreciation separately
  • May leave money on the table for expensive vehicles
✅ Best for: Most gig workers, especially delivery drivers

Actual Expense Method

Real costs × business use percentage

  • Can deduct gas, insurance, repairs, registration, tires
  • Can include depreciation (Section 179, bonus depreciation)
  • Better for expensive vehicles with high operating costs
  • Better when vehicle is used almost 100% for business
  • Must track every expense with receipts all year
  • Must track total vs. business miles to calculate %
  • Can't switch back to standard mileage later
  • Much more complex — may need a CPA
⚠️ Best for: High-cost vehicles used almost exclusively for business
✅ For 90% of gig workers, the standard mileage rate wins. It's simpler, requires less recordkeeping, and produces a higher deduction in most cases because gig workers drive a lot of miles in relatively affordable vehicles. Run both calculations in tax software to compare — TurboTax and H&R Block do this automatically.

What You Can Still Deduct With Standard Mileage

Using the standard mileage rate doesn't mean you can only deduct mileage. These are additional deductions allowed on top of the standard mileage rate:

  • Parking fees — while parked for a business purpose (not at your home)
  • Tolls — any toll paid during a business trip
  • Loan interest on a vehicle loan (the business-use percentage)

Mileage Tracking

How to Track Your Miles — IRS Requirements

The IRS requires a contemporaneous mileage log — meaning you record miles at the time of each trip, not reconstructed from memory months later. An audit without adequate records means the deduction gets denied, even if you actually drove those miles.

What Your Mileage Log Must Include

For each trip (or daily summary for gig workers with many short trips), record:

  • Date of each trip
  • Destination or business purpose
  • Starting odometer reading
  • Ending odometer reading
  • Miles driven for that trip

You also need your odometer reading on January 1 and December 31 of the tax year to document your total annual mileage (used to calculate your business-use percentage).

Filing Your Taxes

How to Claim the Mileage Deduction on Your Tax Return

1

Total your business miles for the year

Add up all your deductible business miles from your mileage log or tracking app. Note your total miles driven for the year (all purposes) — you'll need both numbers for Schedule C Part IV.

2

Multiply by the 2026 rate

Business miles × $0.725 = your mileage deduction. If you have parking fees or tolls, add those separately — they're deductible in addition to the standard rate.

3

Enter on Schedule C, Line 9

On Schedule C (Form 1040), go to Part II — Expenses. Line 9 is "Car and truck expenses." Enter your total mileage deduction here. Tax software will prompt you for all the details.

4

Complete Schedule C Part IV

Part IV asks about your vehicle: date first used for business, total 2026 miles, business miles, commuting miles, other miles, and whether you have written evidence. Answer honestly — this section is what the IRS checks in an audit.

5

Keep your records for 3 years

Don't file your mileage log — just keep it. The IRS has 3 years from your filing date to audit your return. Store your log (physical or digital) and any supporting receipts for at least 3 years after you file.

📌 Using tax software? TurboTax, H&R Block, and TaxSlayer all have dedicated mileage entry sections under "Vehicle Expenses" on Schedule C. They walk you through each question step by step and calculate the deduction automatically. You never need to enter the rate manually.
