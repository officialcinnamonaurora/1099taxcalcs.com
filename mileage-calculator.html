What Counts

Which Miles Can You Deduct?

Not every mile you drive counts as a business deduction. The IRS is specific about what qualifies. Here's exactly what gig workers can and cannot deduct:

✅ Miles You CAN Deduct

  • ✅Miles driven while carrying a passenger (Uber/Lyft)
  • ✅Miles driven to pick up a passenger (deadhead miles)
  • ✅Miles driving to pick up a delivery order
  • ✅Miles while delivering food, groceries, or packages
  • ✅Miles driving to a client meeting or job site
  • ✅Miles to buy business supplies
  • ✅Miles between two different work locations in the same day
  • ✅Miles driving to pick up a rental car for work

❌ Miles You CANNOT Deduct

  • ❌Your daily commute from home to a regular workplace
  • ❌Personal errands run during a work day
  • ❌Miles while the app is off and you're not working
  • ❌Driving to get food or coffee for yourself
  • ❌Vacation or personal travel
  • ❌Miles if you're also claiming actual vehicle expenses for the same car
  • ❌Miles for a vehicle you don't own (rental, borrowed)
  • ❌Commuting from home to your first pickup (some dispute this — log everything)
⚠️ The "first mile" rule for gig workers: The IRS says your commute from home to your first job location is NOT deductible. However, once you accept your first trip/order, miles from that point onward are deductible. Many Uber and DoorDash drivers log ALL miles from the moment they turn the app on — keep detailed records and consult a CPA on your specific situation.

Standard Mileage vs. Actual Expense Method

You have two options for deducting vehicle costs. You must choose one method per vehicle per year and cannot switch mid-year:

MethodHow It WorksBest ForRecord Keeping
Standard Mileage (72.5¢) Multiply total business miles × $0.725 Most gig workers, high mileage drivers Just log your miles (date, miles, purpose)
Actual Expense Method Track real costs: gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation × business-use % Low-mileage drivers with expensive vehicles Every receipt for the entire year
✅ For almost every Uber, DoorDash, and Instacart driver: the standard mileage method wins. You drive a lot, so 72.5¢ × high miles = large deduction without any receipt tracking beyond your mileage log.

⚠️ Uber and DoorDash do NOT track all your miles. Their apps only count miles while you have an active trip — not deadhead miles (driving to pick up), time between orders, or miles after you drop off. A separate tracking app captures 20–40% more miles that the platform misses.
